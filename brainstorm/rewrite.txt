Requirements (in terms of conveying understanding):

  1. **Massively parallel:** G to C scoring.
  2. **Dependencies:** i.e. chair-like qualities, to chair, to living room, to house standard
  3. **Aggregation:** Count constraints that are satisfied, compare to constraints that are not.
  4. **Fuzzy:** Partial scores
  5. **Realism:** Functionally workable example
  
Ideas:
  1. **Diagnosis:** Observation of symptoms --> chance of disease --> chance of intervention
  2. **Natural language:** 
    Natural language graph --> alignment to classes --> repetition / broader contextual understanding --> agent policy decisions --> fuzziness
    Note: basic idea is that the alignment to the ontology is important, because the ontology is how policies are determined 
      a) natural language bag of words / semi structured / relations and qualities
      b) mapping of atoms / leaves to ontology
      c) inference over ontology
      d) scored / aggregate-based
      e) at scale: all n to all C
      f) repeated inference, at scale, ALL IN ONE PASS
      g) threshold vs fuzzy: possibility of ranking results, and seeing explanations, vs keeping them fuzzy
      h) true fuzzy reasoning: possible via reverse. non-monotone via fixpoint application.

## possible concretizations:

my current ideas are basically along the lines of:
- an initial KG is parsed from user text, and assembled into graph form. there's some basic entities, relations, qualities, etc. but the overall structure is not really aligned to any particular ontology
- there is a mapping of some of these terms to parts of an ontology. the ontology is richly annotated with OWL DL and is used to determine logical decision-making
- the fuzzy typed path matching is used to enrich the initial KG -- upgrade it so that the entities are aligned to specific classes from the ontology, thus enabling reasoning to determine a response.

there's other relevant scenarios but these are the ones that come most easily to me 

possible concrete domains include:
- a user describes symptoms. the KG is mapped to OBO foundry ontologies -- and reasoning over these ontologies determines a recommended intervention.
  - pro: real-world application. real, rich ontologies (OBO). 
  - con: high-stakes domain. highly studied. and doesn't necessarily make the best use of the multihop / path aspect
- user is speaking with an NPC in a video game (or "simulation"). the NPC has certain policies about what actions it should take based off of what the user says or does, or what information the NPC learns. e.g.: the NPC is demanding payment to pass a bridge: player can attempt to bluff the NPC to allow passage, can intimidate the NPC, can try making a plea of desperation, logical argument, etc. the game takes the user's KG -> determines likely user intent  -> determines relevant sub-elements the player is referencing wrt that intent -> reasons over the KG to determine the agent's response, according to the policy defined in the ontology -> response KG is passed off to an NLG system that generates it + game actions that the NPC now takes
  - pro: especially relevant due to: relative fuzziness / more important to assume something rather than nothing at all, agent-based decision-making supported by ontologies, RUNTIME CONSTRAINTS / ability to run on player's hardware, importance of translating game designers intent into an action (as opposed to uncontrollable LLM-generated responses), grounding in context, natural extension of the argument, etc. 
    - also note that this was the original context in which i first invented this method: games / procedurally generated content. the pure PCG conceptual blending approach also applies but i kind of like this one too. 
  - con: i dont think my advisor approves of the video game argument........
    - i would try to think of something less video-gamey... but all the best examples are the more video gamey ones.

- user is entering in text of an incident report. the extracted KG is mapped to some ontology for data observations (contrived / existing only for this example). based on the observation, the observation data is entered into the KB correctly, enabling researchers to perform mass search observations at a very particular level
  - an aside: this is still at a fairly vague level, but the issue is the most concrete example i can think of is also the SILLIEST one that could not possibly be taken seriously. i'll put it here to see if we can think of something along the same lines, but actually acceptable for publication in a serious venue:
    - the user is entering in a reported sighting of a paranormal incident. the user has perhaps observed something: a shadowy, bipedal figure in the dark. hints of fur. fast movement. they also saw: gleaming eyes. large claw-like paws. hints of a snout-like face.
    - the system aligns their report to a paranormal activity ontology. from the user's description, it ranks highly as a possible observation of Bigfoot -- but there are various things observed that do not match the accepted myth, and various other things in the myth that were not observed. various inferences are made to store the data more accurately: observation of a biped (bigfoot / yeti / swamp man etc fall under this). nighttime observation. obsevation of an instance of Biped class with fur. etc.
    - later: a paranormal researcher is searching for things that might be observations of bigfoot. they search broadly for observations of bigfoot, and this ranks fairly highly. but they are interested in a specific theory: that various claimed observations of bigfoot are actually observations of bears. they narrow  their search results to specifically bear-like aspects in these observations, and this observation (along with others) fall under that purview. the researcher instead alters the scope of their search to include only observations that DON'T match bear-like entities; the number of results returns falls significantly, but there are still a few important types of observation the researcher can now inspect (essentially, the fringe of their theory). 
  - pro: obviously the above is quite silly, but it is strong in a couple aspects in particular: "fuzzy" determination of what a class should or should not be. differences among different observations. a fundamental inequality: "observation with qualities of a thing != the thing" that show usefulness of precision about semantics. the class is not known ahead of time -- instead, all instances are scored against all classes. the possibility of extending the example to show multi-hop reasoning: if [n] is an observation of [C], then what are they chances that [m] -p-> [n] is a [B] -P-> [C]? (i.e. as you learn things in the "leaves" of the graphs, you can use that information for the roots -- all in a single pass, as long as the constraint DAG is acyclic).
